FIRE stands for Financial Independence, Retire Early. Explore the common variants below — once you calculate your plan, we'll flag the one your numbers currently match.

The safe withdrawal rate (4% rule)

The "4% rule" comes from historical studies (notably the Trinity Study) suggesting a portfolio of roughly 60–75% stocks / 25–40% bonds can support a 4% initial withdrawal, adjusted for inflation each year, over a 30-year retirement without running out — in most historical periods. It is a planning heuristic, not a guarantee:

  • Longer retirements (very early retirees with 40–50+ year horizons) often use a more conservative 3–3.5% instead.
  • Sequence-of-returns risk — a market downturn in your first few retirement years — is the biggest threat to a fixed withdrawal plan. Holding 1–2 years of cash/bonds as a buffer, or flexing spending down in bad years, both help.
  • The FIRE number above is calculated as annual spending needed (gross of taxes) ÷ withdrawal rate.

Strategies to get there faster

  • Raise your savings rate. Savings rate — not income alone — is the single biggest lever on how fast you reach FIRE; cutting your largest expenses (housing, transportation) usually moves the needle more than cutting small recurring purchases.
  • Max tax-advantaged accounts first. See the Tax Savings Strategies page for a fuller menu of accounts and tactics.
  • Consider geographic arbitrage. Retiring in a state with no income tax or generous retirement-income exclusions (see the comparison above) can meaningfully lower your FIRE number.
  • Plan the healthcare bridge. Retiring before 65 means bridging to Medicare via ACA marketplace plans, COBRA, a part-time job with benefits (Barista FIRE), or a spouse's plan.
  • Build a withdrawal order strategy. See the Tax Deep-Dive section above to compare account-withdrawal orderings for your own numbers.
  • Revisit the plan regularly. Update your inputs at least yearly, and after any major life change — small changes in return assumptions or spending compound significantly over a multi-decade horizon.
